Life sciences companies are doing more qualifying R&D work than most realize.

AI-driven drug discovery. Nanotech-based delivery systems. Cell therapy manufacturing. 3D bioprinting for tissue engineering. These aren't future concepts — they're happening now, and the work behind them often qualifies for federal and state R&D tax credits.

Here's what the IRS looks at. The four-part test asks whether your work involves:

→ A permitted purpose (new or improved product, process, formula, or technique)
→ Technological uncertainty (you didn't know if it would work, how it would work, or what the right design would be)
→ A process of experimentation (systematic trial and error, modeling, simulation, testing)
→ Technical in nature (rooted in engineering, biology, chemistry, computer science, or similar disciplines)

If your team is running experiments to develop novel therapies, optimizing manufacturing processes for biologics, building computational models for molecular behavior, or validating new delivery mechanisms — that's exactly the kind of work the credit was designed to support.

Qualifying costs typically include wages for scientists and engineers, supplies consumed during experimentation, and payments to contract research organizations.

The part most companies miss: you don't need to be inventing something entirely new. Improving an existing process, adapting a known method to a new application, or resolving uncertainty in scaling from bench to production — all of that can qualify.

If your life sciences company is investing in technical development and hasn't evaluated the R&D tax credit, it's worth a conversation.
